<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">BEFORE YOU BEGIN
──  IF YOU ARE EXPLORING THE FAITH
You may be holding this booklet because someone who loves you gave it to you. Or maybe you walked into a church and something stirred inside you. Perhaps you are skeptical. Perhaps you are curious. Perhaps you are desperate.
Whatever brought you here, you are welcome. This guide does not assume you have made up your mind about Jesus. It assumes you are willing to look honestly at what He said, what He did, and what He offers. Read with an open mind. Ask hard questions. Let the Scriptures speak for themselves.
If at any point during these nine weeks you come to the place where you want to place your faith in Jesus, Week 1 will show you exactly what that means and how to respond.
──  IF YOU HAVE RECENTLY COME TO FAITH
Something has happened to you. You have turned from sin and placed your faith in Jesus Christ as Lord and Savior. The God of the universe has forgiven you, adopted you, and given you new life. That is the most significant event that will ever happen to you.
But now what? God did not save you to leave you stranded. He saved you to transform you, to walk with you, and to use you for the good of others and the glory of His name. This guide will help you take your first steps with clarity and confidence.
──  HOW TO USE THIS GUIDE
Each week covers one essential area of the Christian life. You can work through this on your own, but it was designed to be walked through with another person—a mentor, a pastor, a friend, or a small group.
Every week includes:
An opening reflection — a real-life scenario connecting the topic to everyday experience.
Core Scripture passages — the biblical foundation for everything you will learn.
Guided teaching — an explanation of what the Scriptures teach and how they shape your life.
Reflection questions — to help you think honestly about what you’ve read.
A practical tool — a specific habit or resource to begin using that week.
Space for notes — room to write, pray, and process what God is doing in you.
──  THE M.A.T.U.R.E. FRAMEWORK
Scripture identifies several markers of a maturing believer. This guide is organized around six of them:
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">M.A.T.U.R.E.
M — Makes Disciples. A growing believer helps others know and follow Jesus (Matthew 28:19–20).
A — Abides in the Word. A growing believer reads, understands, and obeys God’s Word (Psalm 1:1–3; John 15:7).
T — Treasures Community. A growing believer is committed to the local church (Hebrews 10:24–25; Acts 2:42–47).
U — Uses Their Gifts. A growing believer serves others with Spirit-given abilities (1 Peter 4:10; Romans 12:4–8).
R — Relies on the Holy Spirit. A growing believer depends on the Spirit for power and transformation (Galatians 5:16–25).
E — Embraces Accountability. A growing believer invites correction and lives transparently (Proverbs 27:17; James 5:16).
These are not programs to join. They describe the Christ-shaped life God is building in you—week by week, day by day, for the rest of your life.
This is not a course to complete. It is a life to begin.

The Best News in the World
Understanding the Gospel and Receiving a New Identity
──  OPENING REFLECTION
Imagine a man who has been in a dark room his entire life. He has never seen sunlight, never felt warmth on his face. Then one day, someone opens the door. Light floods in. Everything changes—not because the man did anything, but because someone came for him.
That is a picture of the gospel. Whether you are just beginning to explore who Jesus is, or you recently placed your faith in Him, this is where the journey begins.

──  WHAT IS THE GOSPEL?
The word “gospel” means “good news.” But to understand why it is good news, you have to understand the problem it solves.
The Problem: Sin
The Bible teaches that every human being has sinned—we have all thought, spoken, and acted in ways that violate God’s perfect standard (Romans 3:23). Sin is not just doing bad things. It is a condition of the human heart. We were born into it. It separates us from God, and the consequence of sin is death—both physical and spiritual (Romans 6:23).
This is not a popular idea, but it is an honest one. Every human being knows, deep down, that something is broken. The Bible names what it is.
The Solution: Jesus Christ
God did not leave us in our sin. Out of love, He sent His Son, Jesus Christ, to live the perfect life we could not live and to die the death we deserved. On the cross, Jesus took the full penalty for sin upon Himself. Three days later, He rose from the dead, proving that sin and death had been defeated (1 Corinthians 15:3–4).
The Response: Faith and Repentance
Faith means trusting in Jesus—not just believing facts about Him, but depending on Him fully for your salvation. Repentance means turning away from sin and turning toward God. It is a change of mind that leads to a change of life.
If you have never placed your faith in Jesus, you can do so right now. Tell God that you recognize your sin, that you believe Jesus died and rose for you, and that you are placing your trust in Him alone. If you pray that sincerely, everything that follows in this guide is now true of you.
──  WHAT IS NOW TRUE OF YOU IN CHRIST
You are forgiven. Every sin—past, present, and future—has been paid for by the blood of Christ (Colossians 1:13–14).
You are a new creation. God has not improved the old you. He has made you new from the inside out (2 Corinthians 5:17).
You are a child of God. You have been adopted into God’s family (John 1:12; Romans 8:15–16).
You are free from condemnation. There is no guilt hanging over you. Christ has removed it (Romans 8:1).
These truths do not depend on how you feel. They depend on what Christ has done.

You will fail. You will sin. Grace means God’s hold on you is not dependent on your grip on Him. He who began a good work in you will carry it to completion (Philippians 1:6).

<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">WEEK TWO
Hearing God’s Voice
How to Read, Understand, and Live the Bible
──  OPENING REFLECTION
Imagine receiving a handwritten letter from the most important person in the world—written specifically with you in mind. You would not skim it. You would read it carefully, slowly, and probably more than once.
The Bible is the living Word of the God who made you, saved you, and is transforming you. This week, you will learn how to read it, understand it, and let it shape your life.
──  CORE SCRIPTURES
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of God may be complete, equipped for every good work.”
— 2 Timothy 3:16–17
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“For the word of God is living and active, sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing to the division of soul and of spirit, of joints and of marrow, and discerning the thoughts and intentions of the heart.”
— Hebrews 4:12
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Blessed is the man who walks not in the counsel of the wicked, nor stands in the way of sinners, nor sits in the seat of scoffers; but his delight is in the law of the LORD, and on his law he meditates day and night.”
— Psalm 1:1–2
──  WHAT IS THE BIBLE?
The Bible is God’s written revelation of Himself. It is sixty-six books, written over approximately 1,500 years by more than forty authors under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit. Despite this diversity, the Bible tells one unified story: God’s plan to redeem a people for Himself through Jesus Christ.
The Old Testament (39 books) tells the story of creation, the fall, and God’s covenant with Israel. It points forward to a coming Savior.
The New Testament (27 books) records Jesus’ life, death, and resurrection, the birth of the church, and instructions for believers until Christ returns.
Types of Literature
Narrative/History — stories of real people and events (Genesis, Exodus, Acts).
Law — God’s instructions to Israel (Leviticus, Deuteronomy).
Poetry &amp; Wisdom — songs, prayers, and reflections (Psalms, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes).
Prophecy — messages from God through His prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Revelation).
Epistles (Letters) — letters to churches and individuals (Romans, Philippians, 1 Timothy).
Gospels — the four accounts of Jesus’ life (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John). These are the center of the entire Bible.
Chapters and Verses
“John 3:16” means the book of John, chapter 3, verse 16. “Romans 8:1–4” means Romans chapter 8, verses 1 through 4. These divisions were added later for convenience, but they help you find and discuss specific passages.
──  THE SPIRITUAL DISCIPLINE OF BIBLE READING
Regular Bible reading is not optional. It is one of the primary spiritual disciplines—a practice through which God shapes you into the image of Christ. Hebrews 4:12 describes the Bible as “living and active.” When you open Scripture, the Holy Spirit works through it to speak to your heart, expose sin, comfort pain, and direct your steps.
But reading the Bible is not enough. You need a method that helps you slow down, understand, and respond. That is where the HEAR method comes in.
──  THE HEAR JOURNALING METHOD
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">H.E.A.R.
H — HIGHLIGHT. After reading a passage, identify one or two verses that stand out. Write them down. This is the verse the Holy Spirit is drawing your attention to.
E — EXPLAIN. In your own words, explain what this verse means in context. Who is writing? Who is the audience? What does this say about God, about people, or about the gospel? Understanding the meaning is essential before application.
A — APPLY. How does this truth apply to your life right now? Is there something to believe, obey, repent of, or thank God for? Be specific. Vague application produces vague obedience.
R — RESPOND. Write a short prayer to God in response. Thank Him, confess to Him, ask for help, or surrender an area of your life. This is where Bible reading becomes conversation with God.
The HEAR method works with any passage. Keep a journal dedicated to this practice. Over time, you will build a personal record of how God has spoken to you—one of the greatest treasures of your spiritual life.
──  WHERE TO START
Start with the Gospel of John. It was written so that you “may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God” (John 20:31). Read one chapter per day. Use HEAR as you go. Five minutes with a HEAR journal will shape you more than thirty minutes of unfocused reading.

<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">WEEK THREE
Learning to Talk with God
Prayer, the Holy Spirit, and the Disciplines That Shape You
──  OPENING REFLECTION
Think about the closest relationship in your life. What makes it close? Regular, honest communication. You talk. You listen. You share what is really going on.
When you became a Christian—or as you explore what that might mean—you entered a relationship with the living God. He has given you extraordinary gifts to sustain that relationship: prayer, the Holy Spirit, and the spiritual disciplines.
──  CORE SCRIPTURES
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus.”
— Philippians 4:6–7
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Likewise the Spirit helps us in our weakness. For we do not know what to pray for as we ought, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us with groanings too deep for words.”
— Romans 8:26
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“But the Helper, the Holy Spirit, whom the Father will send in my name, he will teach you all things and bring to your remembrance all that I have said to you.”
— John 14:26
──  WHAT IS PRAYER?
Prayer is talking with God. It is not a ritual or a performance. It is a conversation between a child and a Father who loves to hear from you. God wants your honesty, not your eloquence.
But while prayer is simple, it does have shape. One of the most helpful tools for developing a Scripture-fed, Spirit-led, and worship-based prayer life is the 4x4 Pattern of Prayer.
──  THE 4X4 PATTERN OF PRAYER
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">THE 4X4 PATTERN OF PRAYER
1. REVERENCE (Upward). Praise God for who He is and what He has done. This is where prayer begins—not with your needs, but with His nature. Let the Scriptures you are reading fuel your praise (Psalm 145:3; Psalm 100:4–5).
2. RESPONSE (Downward). Respond to who God is in repentance, obedience, and reliance on the Holy Spirit. After praising God, let His character expose where your life does not align. Confess sin. Surrender control (1 John 1:9; Psalm 32:5; Galatians 5:16).
3. REQUESTS (Inward). Ask God for the relationships and resources you need. Bring your needs, desires, fears, and struggles before Him—not because He doesn’t know, but because He wants you to depend on Him (Philippians 4:6–7; Matthew 7:7–8).
4. READINESS (Outward). Ask God to empower and equip you for discipleship and mission to reach every man, woman, and child. Pray for the lost by name. Pray for your church, for the nations, and for boldness (Acts 1:8; 1 Timothy 2:1–4; Colossians 4:2–4).
You do not have to use all four directions every time. But a prayer life that regularly moves upward in reverence, downward in response, inward in requests, and outward in readiness will be full, balanced, and deeply connected to God’s heart.
──  PRAYING THE BIBLE
One of the most transformative practices you can develop is learning to pray the Bible itself. Many believers struggle with prayer because they run out of things to say. Praying the Bible solves that problem.
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">HOW TO PRAY THE BIBLE
Step 1: Open a passage of Scripture. The Psalms are the best place to start (Psalm 23, 27, 139).
Step 2: Read slowly, one verse or phrase at a time.
Step 3: After each verse, pause and talk to God about what it says. If the verse says “The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want,” you might pray: “God, You are my shepherd. Help me trust that You will provide. Forgive me for the anxiety I’ve been carrying.”
Step 4: When a verse does not prompt anything, move to the next. No pressure to pray about every line.
Step 5: Continue until your time is up. Depth matters more than distance.
──  WHO IS THE HOLY SPIRIT?
The Holy Spirit is not an impersonal force. He is the third Person of the Trinity—fully God. When you believed in Jesus, the Holy Spirit came to live inside you (Ephesians 1:13–14).
He teaches you (John 14:26). He convicts you (John 16:8). He empowers you (Galatians 5:16–22). He intercedes for you (Romans 8:26–27). He produces fruit in you — love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control (Galatians 5:22–23).
──  THE SPIRITUAL DISCIPLINES
Spiritual disciplines are God-given practices through which the Spirit shapes you. They are not ways to earn favor—you already have that. They are means of experiencing His presence and being transformed:
Bible reading &amp; meditation (Psalm 1:2; Joshua 1:8).
Prayer — adoration, confession, supplication, intercession.
Fasting — voluntarily going without food to focus on God (Matthew 6:16–18).
Fellowship — gathering with believers for worship and accountability (Hebrews 10:24–25).
Giving — stewarding resources generously as worship (2 Corinthians 9:7).
Solitude &amp; silence — withdrawing from noise to be alone with God (Mark 1:35; Luke 5:16).
Worship — declaring God’s worth through song, obedience, and a surrendered life (Romans 12:1–2).
Start with Bible reading and prayer. Add others as you grow.

You Were Made for Family
The Church, Biblical Community, and the Ordinances
──  OPENING REFLECTION
A coal pulled from a fire will quickly grow cold. But surrounded by other coals, it stays hot. God did not design the Christian life to be lived in isolation. You were saved into a family.
──  CORE SCRIPTURES
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“And they devoted themselves to the apostles’ teaching and the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers.”
— Acts 2:42
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“And let us consider how to stir one another up to love and good works, not neglecting to meet together, as is the habit of some, but encouraging one another.”

— 1 Corinthians 12:12
──  WHY THE CHURCH MATTERS
The church is not a building or a weekend service. It is the people of God—saved by Jesus and gathered to worship, grow, and serve. The Bible calls the church the “body of Christ” (1 Corinthians 12:27), the “household of God” (Ephesians 2:19), and the “bride of Christ” (Ephesians 5:25–27). Belonging to a local church is not optional. It is part of following Him.
In Acts 2:42–47, the first Christians devoted themselves to four things: the apostles’ teaching, fellowship, the breaking of bread, and prayer. This was not a program. It was a lifestyle.
──  WHAT BIBLICAL COMMUNITY LOOKS LIKE
Honesty — sharing what is really going on (James 5:16).
Encouragement — speaking truth and hope into others’ lives (1 Thessalonians 5:11).
Accountability — giving others permission to challenge you toward obedience (Proverbs 27:17).
Sacrificial love — serving at cost to yourself (1 John 3:16–18).
──  BAPTISM: YOUR PUBLIC DECLARATION
Baptism does not save you—faith in Christ does. But baptism is your public declaration that you have died with Christ and been raised to new life (Romans 6:3–4). When you go under the water, it symbolizes your old life buried with Jesus. When you come up, it symbolizes new life. Jesus commanded His followers to baptize new disciples (Matthew 28:19).
──  COMMUNION: REMEMBERING WHAT HE DID
On the night before He was crucified, Jesus took bread and said, “This is my body, given for you. Do this in remembrance of me.” He took the cup and said, “This cup is the new covenant in my blood” (Luke 22:19–20).
Remembering His sacrifice. Proclaiming the gospel (1 Corinthians 11:26). Examining your heart (1 Corinthians 11:28).

The God Who Walks with You Through Fire
Suffering, Trials, and the Faithfulness of God
──  OPENING REFLECTION
Following Jesus does not mean your life will get easier. In some ways it may get harder. You may face misunderstanding, loss, or confusion. This is normal. It is not a sign that something has gone wrong. Suffering is part of the Christian life. And remarkably, God uses it.
──  CORE SCRIPTURES
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds, for you know that the testing of your faith produces steadfastness.”
— James 1:2–3
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character, and character produces hope.”
— Romans 5:3–4
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“In the world you will have tribulation. But take heart; I have overcome the world.”
— John 16:33
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose.”
— Romans 8:28
──  WHY SUFFERING EXISTS
Suffering entered through sin. When Adam and Eve rejected God, the created order fractured (Genesis 3). But God entered this broken world. Jesus experienced hunger, rejection, betrayal, and death. He does not look at your suffering from a distance (Hebrews 4:15).
──  WHAT GOD DOES THROUGH SUFFERING
Suffering refines your faith — trials forge deep confidence in God (1 Peter 1:6–7; James 1:2–4).
Suffering produces character — endurance, character, hope. You cannot develop endurance without something to endure (Romans 5:3–5).
Suffering draws you closer to God — in pain, you cling to God in ways you never would otherwise (Psalm 34:18).
Suffering equips you to comfort others — your pain becomes a resource for ministry (2 Corinthians 1:3–4).
──  HOW TO ENDURE
Bring your pain to God honestly. The Psalms give permission to cry out (Psalm 13; Psalm 42).
Stay anchored in the Word. When emotions are loud, let Scripture be louder.

Fix your eyes on eternity. “This light momentary affliction is preparing for us an eternal weight of glory” (2 Corinthians 4:17).

<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">WEEK SIX
The War You’re Already In
Sin, Temptation, and Spiritual Warfare
──  OPENING REFLECTION
Now that you belong to Christ, you may have noticed the fight has intensified. Old habits pull at you. You are in a war. Not because something is wrong with you, but because something is right. Greater is He who is in you than he who is in the world (1 John 4:4). You are fighting from a victory already won.
──  CORE SCRIPTURES
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“No temptation has overtaken you that is not common to man. God is faithful, and he will not let you be tempted beyond your ability, but with the temptation he will also provide the way of escape.”
— 1 Corinthians 10:13
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“For we do not wrestle against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the cosmic powers over this present darkness.”
— Ephesians 6:12
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Submit yourselves therefore to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you.”
— James 4:7
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness.”
— 1 John 1:9
──  KNOW YOUR ENEMIES
1. The World
The system of values operating apart from God. It pressures you to chase comfort, status, and pleasure (Romans 12:2; 1 John 2:15–17). The antidote is a renewed mind through Scripture.
2. The Flesh
Sinful desires that remain within you after salvation. You are new in Christ but not yet perfect (Galatians 5:17). You overcome by walking in the Spirit, confessing quickly, and replacing sinful habits with godly ones (Ephesians 4:22–24).
3. The Devil
A real fallen angel whose primary weapon is deception (1 Peter 5:8). He lies about God, about you, and about sin. You resist by knowing and speaking Scripture—exactly as Jesus did in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1–11; James 4:7).
──  HOW TO FIGHT
Stay in the Word — it is your sword (Ephesians 6:17).
Pray constantly — access God’s power in real time.
Confess quickly — sin grows in secrecy and dies in the light (James 5:16).
Flee temptation — leave the room, delete the app, avoid the situation (2 Timothy 2:22).
Put on the armor of God — truth, righteousness, gospel, faith, salvation, the Word, and prayer (Ephesians 6:10–18).
──  WHEN YOU FALL
You will sin. When you do, you do not lose your salvation. First John 1:9 promises that confession restores fellowship immediately. Repentance is not a one-time event. It is a lifestyle. Every time you turn back to God, He is waiting—not with anger, but with grace.

3.  Do you have someone you can confess sin to honestly? If not, who could that be?
 
 
 
4.  Read Ephesians 6:10–18. Which piece of armor do you most need this week?

<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">WEEK SEVEN
Open Hands
Generosity, Stewardship, and Living for What Lasts
──  OPENING REFLECTION
A child who has always been hungry hoards food when it appears. But when placed in a home where the table is always full, something shifts. The grip loosens. Generosity is not about having enough to give. It is about trusting the One who has given you everything.
──  CORE SCRIPTURES
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“The earth is the LORD’s and the fullness thereof, the world and those who dwell therein.”
— Psalm 24:1
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Honor the LORD with your wealth and with the firstfruits of all your produce.”
— Proverbs 3:9
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Each one must give as he has decided in his heart, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver.”
— 2 Corinthians 9:7
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also.”
— Matthew 6:21
──  EVERYTHING IS GOD’S
You do not own anything. Psalm 24:1 declares everything belongs to God. You are a steward—someone who manages what belongs to another. The question becomes: “How does God want me to use what He has entrusted to me?”
──  PRINCIPLES OF GENEROUS LIVING
Give first, not last — honor God with the firstfruits (Proverbs 3:9).
Give cheerfully — generosity flows from gratitude, not obligation (2 Corinthians 9:7).
Give sacrificially — generosity that costs nothing reveals little about your heart (Mark 12:41–44).
Give to the church and those in need — support ministry and care for the poor (Malachi 3:10; Galatians 2:10).
When you give, you are worshiping. You are declaring that God is the source of everything and His Kingdom is worth investing in.
──  REFLECTION QUESTIONS
1.  How does the idea of stewardship change how you think about your possessions?
 
 
 
2.  Is your giving firstfruits or leftovers?
 
 
 
3.  Where do you notice anxiety about money? What does it reveal?
 
 
 
4.  Read Matthew 6:19–21. What earthly treasures are you tempted to cling to?
 
 
 
──  THIS WEEK’S TOOL
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">A FIRST STEP IN GIVING
Step 1: Decide on an amount. Start with what requires trust.
Step 2: Give it first—before bills, before extras.
Step 3: Pray over it. Ask God to use it and grow your trust.
The goal is not a number—it is a heart that holds everything with open hands.

<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">WEEK EIGHT
Built to Serve
Discovering and Using Your Spiritual Gifts
──  OPENING REFLECTION
When a baby is born, every part exists to serve the whole body. When you were born again, God gave you gifts—Spirit-empowered abilities—not for your personal benefit alone, but for the building up of the body of Christ. You are not a spectator. You are equipped for a role no one else can fill.
──  CORE SCRIPTURES
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“As each has received a gift, use it to serve one another, as good stewards of God’s varied grace.”
— 1 Peter 4:10
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“Having gifts that differ according to the grace given to us, let us use them.”
— Romans 12:6
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“God arranged the members in the body, each one of them, as he chose.”
— 1 Corinthians 12:18
──  WHAT ARE SPIRITUAL GIFTS?
Abilities given by the Holy Spirit for serving others and building up the church. The NT lists include teaching, serving, encouraging, giving, leading, mercy, hospitality, wisdom, faith, administration, evangelism, and shepherding (Romans 12:6–8; 1 Corinthians 12:4–11; Ephesians 4:11–13). Every gift matters. No gift is more important than another.
──  HOW TO DISCOVER YOUR GIFTS
Serve in various ways — pay attention to where you feel energized, effective, and fruitful.
Listen to the body — others often recognize your gifts before you do.
Pray and ask God — the Spirit wants you to discover and use them.
──  GIFTS WITHOUT CHARACTER
Paul’s chapter on love (1 Corinthians 13) sits right in the middle of his teaching on gifts. Love is the context. Gifting without character produces chaos. Character without gifting produces passivity. God wants both.

3.  Is there an area of service you have been avoiding? What would it look like to step in?

Now Go
Making Disciples and the Life That Lies Ahead
──  OPENING REFLECTION
For eight weeks, the question has been, “How do I grow?” That question never stops. But a new question now joins it: “Who else can I help grow?” Jesus did not save you to be a consumer. He saved you to participate in His mission: make disciples.
──  CORE SCRIPTURES
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age.”
— Matthew 28:18–20
<w:tr><w:tc><w:p><w:r><w:t xml:space="preserve">“And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses, entrust to faithful people who will be able to teach others also.”

— Acts 1:8
──  WHAT IS A DISCIPLE?
A follower and learner of Jesus who is being transformed by Him and helping others do the same. Discipleship is not complete until it is reproduced. A disciple who never helps someone else follow Jesus is like a river with no outlet.
──  THE GREAT COMMISSION
It is backed by authority. “All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me.”
It is clear in method. “Make disciples” is the command. Baptizing and teaching are the means.
It comes with a promise. “I am with you always.” He goes with you.
──  YOU ARE READY
You already have everything you need:
Your testimony — no one can argue with your story.
Your gospel summary — the framework you wrote in Week 1.
This guide — everything you have learned is something you can walk someone else through.
──  HOW TO START
Pray for opportunities. Ask God to put people in your path.
Share your story. Be honest. Be clear.
Invite someone to walk with you. Offer to go through this guide with a new believer or seeker.
Keep growing yourself. The best disciplemakers are still being discipled.
──  THE LIFE AHEAD
You now have a foundation. Go. Make disciples. Abide in the Word. Treasure community. Use your gifts. Rely on the Spirit. Embrace accountability.
This is what it looks like to follow Jesus. And it is the most meaningful life you will ever live.
